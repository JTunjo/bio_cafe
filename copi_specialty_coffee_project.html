Overview
Executive Summary

This proposal outlines a structured two-year joint venture between Colombian specialty coffee technologist Javier Eduardo Chacón (Huila, Colombia) and Philippine coffee industry leaders Pablito M. Villegas and Pacita Juan, aimed at transforming Batangas-region Coffea liberica (Barako) and Coffea excelsa into internationally competitive specialty coffee origins.

The Philippines holds one of the world's most distinctive coffee heritages — a country where Liberica and Excelsa coffees represent a cultural identity found nowhere else at commercial scale. Yet this heritage remains largely invisible to international specialty markets. The gap is not one of genetics or terroir; it is one of post-harvest precision, process standardization, and market positioning infrastructure.

Colombia, through decades of research and field-level innovation, has developed some of the world's most advanced post-harvest methodologies — from controlled anaerobic fermentation and precision honey processing to IoT-enabled fermentation monitoring and AI-assisted process optimization. This initiative brings those capabilities directly to Philippine Liberica and Excelsa, with the express goal of creating a globally recognized specialty origin from one of the world's rarest coffee species.

Strategic Proposition
The combination of Colombia's technology leadership in post-harvest science and the Philippines' unparalleled biodiversity in rare coffee species represents a unique, globally differentiated specialty coffee development opportunity — one that, if executed correctly, could position Philippine Liberica and Excelsa among the most sought-after origins in international specialty coffee.

This initiative is structured in three investment phases spanning 24 months, designed to allow staged funding commitments aligned with verifiable deliverables. Phase 1 (Foundation & Pilot) establishes the technical and operational infrastructure. Phase 2 (Scale & Training) expands the implementation to a broader producer network and builds local technical capacity. Phase 3 (Commercialization) prepares export-ready lots, develops market positioning assets, and facilitates access to international specialty coffee channels.

Market & Industry Analysis
Strategic Context & Industry Opportunity

Global Specialty Coffee Trends

The global specialty coffee market has undergone a structural transformation over the past decade. Consumers — particularly in North America, Europe, Australia, Japan, South Korea, and the Middle East — are actively seeking origin differentiation, processing transparency, and sensory complexity beyond what mainstream Arabica can offer. The Third Wave has matured, and a Fourth Wave characterized by experimental processing, rare species, and traceability-driven narratives is now the frontier of premium positioning.

In this context, Coffea liberica and Coffea excelsa occupy an extraordinary position. They are, by definition, rare at the level of global supply. Unlike Arabica — cultivated on nearly every continent — commercially available Liberica is produced almost exclusively in the Philippines and limited areas of West Africa. No other country has the combination of scale, heritage, and institutional knowledge to develop Liberica and Excelsa as a specialty export origin.

Rising Interest in Rare Coffee Species

Specialty roasters and buyers in key import markets are increasingly vocal about their interest in non-Arabica species. The success of Coffea stenophylla revival projects, the Robusta fine-grade movement, and academic publications on climate-resilient coffee species have all contributed to growing buyer appetite for Liberica and Excelsa. Several prominent international specialty roasters have publicly expressed interest in sourcing commercially viable, traceable, high-quality Liberica lots — with no reliable supply currently available at the volumes or quality levels they require.

Market Gap
International specialty buyers are actively seeking Liberica and Excelsa lots. The absence of traceable, precision-processed, export-ready Philippine specialty Liberica in the market is not a demand problem — it is a supply and quality infrastructure problem. This initiative addresses that gap directly.

Climate Resilience Advantages of Liberica

Coffea liberica is inherently more tolerant of heat, humidity, and lower altitudes than Arabica — characteristics of increasing strategic importance as climate change compresses Arabica's viable growing zones globally. The FAO and CGIAR have flagged Liberica as a priority species for climate-resilient coffee production in tropical lowland regions. Positioning Philippine Liberica at the intersection of climate resilience and specialty quality is a contribution to the global coffee sector's long-term sustainability agenda.

Philippine Coffee Heritage and Positioning

The Philippines is one of the few countries in the world to produce all four commercially recognized coffee species: Arabica, Robusta, Liberica (Barako), and Excelsa. Batangas province — and specifically the municipalities of Malvar, Lipa, and Santo Tomas — has been the historic heartland of Barako cultivation, with generational farming families maintaining old-growth trees that represent irreplaceable genetic and cultural heritage.

Despite this extraordinary asset, Philippine specialty coffee exports remain negligible compared to Vietnam, Indonesia, or Papua New Guinea. The primary constraint is not production capacity — it is post-harvest technology, quality consistency, and market access infrastructure. This initiative is designed to resolve precisely these constraints.

Market Differentiation Opportunities

  • Species rarity: True commercial Liberica at specialty grade is essentially unavailable internationally
  • Processing innovation: IoT-monitored fermentation processes applied to Liberica create unique, verifiable flavor profiles
  • Origin narrative: Batangas Barako heritage, combined with Colombian fermentation science, creates a compelling cross-cultural origin story
  • Traceability: IoT data logging enables lot-level traceability — a growing requirement for premium international buyers
  • Climate positioning: Alignment with specialty coffee's growing interest in climate-resilient species

Export Potential

Conservative projections for a fully developed Philippine specialty Liberica export program suggest achievable FOB premiums of USD 8–15 per kilogram above conventional green coffee prices within 36 months of market entry. With Batangas alone estimated to have 300,000+ productive Liberica trees, even a 5% transition to specialty-grade processing represents a commercially meaningful supply base for international buyers.

Technical Work Plan
Scope of Work
Phase A · Pre-Production Assessment

A. Pre-Production Assessment

The foundation of any precision post-harvest program is an accurate understanding of the raw material. Before fermentation protocols are introduced, a systematic pre-production assessment will be conducted across selected farms in Malvar, Lipa, and Cavite municipalities of Batangas.

  • Farm Mapping & Varietal Assessment: GPS-referenced mapping of all participating farms; identification and cataloging of Liberica and Excelsa tree populations, estimated age, density, and productive capacity
  • Maturity Standards Development: Establishment of visual and Brix-meter-based cherry maturity benchmarks specific to Liberica and Excelsa physiological ripening stages
  • Soil Diagnostics: Soil sampling and analysis (pH, organic matter, nitrogen, phosphorus, potassium, and micronutrients) at representative farm sites
  • Pest and Disease Baseline: Assessment of Coffee Berry Borer, leaf rust, and other relevant pest/disease pressures affecting cherry quality at harvest
  • Producer Selection and Engagement: Structured selection of 20–25 initial pilot producers based on tree quality, farm accessibility, water access, and willingness to adopt new protocols
Phase B · Production Optimization

B. Production Optimization

Selective harvesting is the single most impactful quality intervention at the farm level. This phase introduces harvesting protocols that shift the cultural practice from strip-picking to selective ripe-cherry picking.

  • Harvest Protocols: Introduction of selective picking standards with visual maturity guides, producer incentive structures for cherry quality, and field-level sorting training
  • Traceability Systems: Lot identification systems using farm codes, harvest dates, and processing batch numbers — integrated with IoT data logging from post-harvest onwards
  • Quality-Linked Incentive Design: Development of a quality premium payment framework that rewards producers for delivering properly matured, sorted cherry
  • Field Data Collection: Tablet/mobile-based data entry systems for harvest volumes, cherry Brix readings, and delivery records
Phase C · Post-Harvest Technology Transfer & IoT Integration

C. Post-Harvest Technology Transfer & IoT Integration

This is the technical core of the initiative. The post-harvest phase introduces a suite of Colombian-origin processing methodologies adapted through systematic experimentation to the specific physiological characteristics of Liberica and Excelsa — all monitored and documented through an integrated bioreactor and IoT platform.

Processing Systems

  • Controlled Anaerobic Fermentation: CO2-purged hermetic bioreactor fermentation with time, temperature, pH, and Brix monitoring; customized protocols for Liberica's larger cherry and bean size
  • Honey Processing Variants: Yellow, red, and black honey mucilage-retention drying protocols; drying bed design adapted for tropical humidity conditions
  • Extended Natural Processing: Full-fruit drying with controlled temperature management; cherry selection standards for natural processing suitability
  • Washed Processing Optimization: Fermentation tank timing calibrated with pH and temperature probes; mucilage removal standards and clean-water fermentation protocols

IoT-Enabled Monitoring System — Core Technological Differentiator

Integrated Bioreactor & IoT Platform
The project deploys a turnkey integrated bioreactor and IoT monitoring system — the centerpiece of the technical infrastructure at approximately USD 90,000. This system includes pH probes, in-tank and ambient temperature sensors, CO2 and humidity sensors, IoT gateway networks, real-time dashboards, and an AI-assisted data analysis layer for fermentation curve pattern recognition and predictive endpoint detection. All IoT system hardware, software, data platforms, and associated intellectual property remain the sole property of the technology providers selected to execute the project. Fermentation records generated by the system are either jointly owned by the venture partners or made publicly available through co-publications with any academic institutions participating in the implementation.
  • Microbial Management: Inoculant trials with commercial yeast and bacterial cultures selected for Liberica compatibility; microbial diversity assessment through partner laboratory analysis
  • SOP Library: Full Standard Operating Procedures documented for each processing method, calibrated through pilot experimentation and validated against sensory outcomes
Phase D · Quality Assurance & Sensory Development

D. Quality Assurance & Sensory Development

  • Cupping Lab Setup: Establishment of a fully equipped cupping facility at a central Batangas processing site, using SCA-equivalent infrastructure (grinders, brewing equipment, cupping bowls, and sensory reference library). Note: Liberica and Excelsa are not formally evaluated under SCA protocols; all scoring will be conducted by a dedicated panel of North American specialty buyers and Liberica-trained cuppers using an SCA-equivalent scoring methodology adapted for these species.
  • Sensory Panels: Structured cupping panels with trained evaluators at regular intervals; blind cupping protocols for objective lot evaluation
  • Roasting Trials: Sample roasting profiles developed for Liberica and Excelsa using a drum sample roaster; roast curve documentation and correlation with sensory outcomes
  • Green Coffee Grading: Physical defect analysis, moisture and water activity measurement, screen size assessment, and density profiling for all pilot lots
  • Flavor Mapping: Development of a Batangas Liberica/Excelsa flavor lexicon based on cupping data; origin flavor identity documentation for marketing use
Phase E · Capacity Building & Training

E. Capacity Building & Training

Long-term impact requires that the knowledge and skills introduced through this initiative become permanently embedded in the local system — not dependent on continuous external technical support.

  • Producer Workshops: Monthly field workshops covering selective harvesting, cherry handling, fermentation basics, and quality awareness — conducted in Filipino/Tagalog with bilingual materials
  • Technician Certification Program: A structured 80-hour curriculum for post-harvest operators covering fermentation science, IoT system operation, data logging, troubleshooting, and QA protocols
  • Sensory Training: Basic Q-Grader-aligned sensory calibration for local staff and select producers; cupping protocols and defect identification training
  • Training Manuals: Full bilingual (English/Filipino) training manual library covering all aspects of the program; designed for ongoing use after project completion
Phase F · Domestic Market Validation

F. Domestic Market Validation

  • Manila, Cebu, Davao Testing: Structured tasting events in each city, targeting specialty café owners, Q Graders, food and beverage professionals, and informed consumers
  • Café Collaboration Program: Placement of pilot lot samples with 8–10 leading specialty cafés for on-menu trials and staff/customer feedback collection
  • Blind Cupping Events: Competitive blind cuppings with Barako benchmarks and international references — generating objective comparative data
  • Price Acceptance Studies: Consumer willingness-to-pay surveys for specialty-positioned Philippine Liberica at various price points
  • Roasting Profile Adaptation: Collaboration with Manila and Cebu specialty roasters to develop locally optimized roast profiles
Phase G · International Positioning Strategy

G. International Positioning Strategy

  • Export Readiness: Green coffee preparation, moisture stabilization, and export-standard packaging for at least 5 commercial-scale lots (60–100 kg each)
  • Sample Preparation and Distribution: Professionally packaged sample kits with full origin documentation, fermentation data, and cupping notes — shipped to 12+ targeted international specialty roasters and importers
  • Branding and Storytelling: Development of the origin identity: Batangas Liberica/Excelsa visual identity, origin story, producer profiles, and processing narrative
  • Exhibition Readiness: Preparation of booth materials, sample lots, and team representation for at least one international specialty coffee event (SCA Expo, World of Coffee, or equivalent)

The project operates under a formal Joint Venture Agreement (JVA) to be executed prior to the initiation of Phase 1 activities. The JVA defines the respective responsibilities, resource contributions, decision-making authorities, IP ownership arrangements, and commercialization rights of each party.

Reporting Structure

A Joint Management Committee (JMC) — comprising the three named partners and one independent advisor where applicable — meets monthly (virtually) and quarterly (in-person in the Philippines) to review progress, address bottlenecks, approve budget adjustments, and confirm phase transition decisions.

IP and Data Ownership

  • IoT System & Technology Platform: All hardware, software, data platforms, algorithms, and associated intellectual property of the bioreactor and IoT monitoring system remain the sole property of the technology providers selected to execute the project. The joint venture holds a use license for the duration of the program
  • Fermentation Records and Process Data: Records generated through the IoT system are either jointly owned by the venture partners or made publicly available through co-publications with any academic institutions participating in the implementation, as agreed on a case-by-case basis
  • SOPs and Training Materials: Jointly owned operational documentation; bilingual versions may be contributed to Philippine government agricultural extension programs upon project conclusion
  • Flavor Maps and Sensory Data: Jointly owned; may be shared with buyers and researchers under NDA during the commercialization phase
  • Origin Brand Identity: Philippine-owned; Colombian team retains attribution rights as technical partner

Commercialization Rights

Export commercialization of specialty lots produced under the program is the primary right of the Philippine partners and any future export entity established under the JV. The Colombian partner retains the right to reference the program in technical publications and international presentations, subject to prior notification and accuracy review. Revenue sharing arrangements for any branded product lines or international exhibition activities will be defined in the JVA commercial schedules.

Beyond the Project
Long-Term Vision

Scaling Opportunities

The systems, protocols, and trained human capital developed under this project are explicitly designed to be scalable. The bioreactor and IoT platform can accommodate additional farm sites and fermentation batches as the technology provider's licensing model allows. The technician certification program can be expanded into a nationally recognized post-harvest training credential under partnership with TESDA or the Philippine Coffee Board. The SOP library becomes a living document, adaptable to other Philippine coffee regions.

A successful Phase 3 outcome creates the foundation for a five-year commercialization phase targeting annual specialty Liberica and Excelsa export volumes of 5–15 metric tons, generating FOB revenues in the range of USD 120,000–250,000 per year at maturity — exclusively from the Batangas pilot region. Regional replication across CALABARZON, Cavite, and Mindanao Liberica areas would multiply these figures substantially.

Regional Expansion

Batangas is chosen as the entry point for its proximity to Manila, established Liberica heritage, and the institutional networks of the Philippine initiating partners. The technical system developed here is directly transferable to other Liberica-producing regions — including Cavite, Quezon, and the emerging Liberica cultivation areas of Northern Mindanao. The project's documentation and SOP library are designed from the outset with this replication intent.

Philippine Specialty Coffee Identity

Beyond commercial metrics, this initiative has the potential to reshape how the world perceives Philippine coffee. The country's four-species legacy — unique globally — combined with a documented, data-verified precision processing system and a compelling origin narrative, creates a brand positioning that no other coffee-producing country can replicate. Batangas Barako, precision-fermented is not merely a product — it is an expression of Philippine agricultural identity brought to the standards of the global specialty market.

Vision Statement
By 2028, Batangas Liberica and Philippine Excelsa should be recognized names on international specialty coffee menus — not as curiosities, but as premium, traceable, precision-processed origins with documented sensory identities and reliable supply. This project is the foundation on which that recognition is built.
