Abstract

The Philippines is the only country in the world producing Coffea liberica (Barako) and Coffea excelsa at meaningful commercial scale — a species heritage with no parallel globally. Yet these coffees remain almost entirely absent from international specialty markets, not due to lack of quality potential, but due to a persistent gap in post-harvest technology, process standardization, and market positioning infrastructure.

This joint venture closes that gap by deploying Colombia's most advanced post-harvest science — precision fermentation design, controlled anaerobic processing, and an integrated bioreactor and IoT monitoring platform — directly into Batangas-region Liberica and Excelsa farms. The program is led by Colombian specialist Javier Eduardo Chacón in partnership with Philippine coffee industry initiators Pablito M. Villegas and Pacita Juan, targeting a minimum of 15 specialty-grade documented pilot lots, 40 trained producers, and at least 5 export-ready commercial lots within 24 months. The end goal is a Philippine specialty coffee origin — Batangas Liberica and Excelsa — that international roasters can source, tell a story about, and pay a significant premium for.

Discussion Points
Q
Why does this need Colombian expertise — can't Philippine technicians develop these protocols independently?
The specific combination of controlled anaerobic fermentation, precision honey processing, and IoT-integrated real-time monitoring has taken Colombia over a decade of field-level iteration to develop. Replicating that from scratch in the Philippines would require a similar timeline and significant independent R&D investment. This partnership compresses that learning curve to 24 months by importing proven methodology directly — and critically, it transfers the knowledge permanently through certified local technicians and documented SOPs, so the Philippines owns the capability by project end.
Q
Liberica and Excelsa are not scored by SCA standards. How will quality be evaluated and communicated to buyers?
This is an important distinction. Standard SCA cupping protocols and scoring are designed for Arabica and do not translate meaningfully to Liberica or Excelsa. All quality evaluation in this program will be conducted by a dedicated panel of North American specialty buyers and Liberica-trained cuppers using an SCA-equivalent methodology adapted for these species. Point scores referenced in the proposal (e.g., ≥ 84 pts) represent equivalency on this adapted scale — not official SCA scores. This is consistent with how other non-Arabica specialty lots (e.g., fine Robusta) are increasingly evaluated in the market.
Q
Who owns the IoT technology and the fermentation data generated during the program?
The bioreactor hardware, IoT platform software, algorithms, and all associated intellectual property remain the sole property of the technology providers selected to execute the project. The joint venture holds an operational use license for the program duration. Fermentation data and process records generated through the system are either jointly owned by the venture partners or made publicly available through co-publications with any academic institutions participating in the implementation — to be agreed on a case-by-case basis. Operational SOPs, flavor maps, and training materials developed by the team are jointly owned by the Philippine and Colombian partners.
Q
Is the $90,000 bioreactor and IoT system cost justified for a 24-month pilot program?
Yes, for two reasons. First, this is not a consumable — the system is a permanent infrastructure investment that remains in the Philippines after the project ends, enabling future production seasons at no additional capital cost. Second, the IoT platform is what makes the program scientifically credible and commercially differentiated: real-time fermentation monitoring, reproducible batch-to-batch consistency, and AI-assisted process optimization are precisely what converts anecdotal quality improvements into internationally verifiable, buyer-trustworthy claims. A program without this infrastructure would produce good coffee; this system produces documented, traceable, premium coffee.
Q
What happens after the 24-month program ends? Is this a self-sustaining model?
The program is explicitly designed for post-project independence. By Month 24, the Philippines side will have: 6+ certified post-harvest technicians, a complete SOP library in English and Filipino, fully installed processing and quality infrastructure, an established domestic market presence, and an active pipeline of international buyer relationships. A commercialization roadmap for 2028 onwards is a formal project deliverable. The Colombian partner's role transitions from implementation lead to periodic advisory — available for technical consultation and continued market representation, but no longer operationally required for day-to-day production.
Q
Is there international demand for Philippine Liberica right now, or is this creating a market that doesn't exist yet?
Demand exists — supply does not. Multiple prominent international specialty roasters have publicly expressed interest in sourcing traceable, high-quality Liberica, and the species has been featured in specialty coffee media as one of the most anticipated "new" origins. The challenge is that no Philippine producer has yet been able to meet the quality consistency, documentation, and traceability standards required by specialty importers. This program is not a market creation exercise — it is a supply development program for a market that is already asking for the product.
Q
Can the program be replicated in other Philippine Liberica-producing regions after Batangas?
Yes, and this is explicitly part of the long-term vision. Batangas is the entry point because of its established Liberica heritage, proximity to Manila, and the institutional networks of the Philippine initiating partners. The SOP library, training curriculum, and IoT system architecture are all designed from the outset with replication in mind — transferable to Cavite, Quezon, and Northern Mindanao Liberica areas with minimal adaptation. A successful Batangas program creates the proof-of-concept, the trained trainers, and the market relationships needed to scale nationally.
